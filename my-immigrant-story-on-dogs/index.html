My immigrant story on dogs

by Prisca Dorcas Mojica Rodriguez

I am terrified of dogs.

When I see a dog, no matter the size, training, breed, or your incessant affirmations

that he is a “sweet dog”: the world slows down. Shit, the world practically stops, for

me. When a dog is in my general vicinity, everything moves in slow motion and I am

not thinking, I am on survival mode.

In Nicaragua, we learn to have a healthy fear of dogs. Feral dogs are found all over

my native land, and they are starving and a lot of them have rabies. If you are eating

outdoors, in a plaza, or just at a restaurant the odds of a hungry dog approaching are

high, very very very high.

I have a few fuzzy memories that feel like dreams, of me being chased by feral dogs.

You see, when I was younger, nobody really owned housedogs, in Nicaragua.

Scratch that, only the elites owned dogs. Those who did own dogs, and weren’t part

of the ruling class, had them for protection. They had guard dogs, angry mean dogs

that were to instill fear of those trying to steal.

It has taken me a LOOONG time to be comfortable around dogs because I had some

pretty traumatic experiences with dogs that were imprinted into my young mind. I

realize that now, in the USA, fearing dogs makes me an outsider. I know that my

indignation at your carelessness to walk your dog without a leash around your

neighborhood seems trite to you, but I was conditioned to fear dogs so please

respect that as I respect your love of dogs.

My tension around dogs makes your friendly housedogs tense. I know this. I have a

very unfunny memory of being chased by a dog, in my early twenties. And since my

brain goes into survival mode, I remember running inside someone’s open door all

the way into their living room. I scared the living daylights out of that man, whose

house I barged into, but trust me: I was more scared of what that dog would do to

me instead of what this strange man could do to me in his home.

I am now comfortable around this one dog. And when I said “comfortable” I mean, I

do not run in fear nor do I hyperventilate in her presence. She doesn’t jump on me,

and while I genuinely believe that she is capable of annihilating me in one swift bite,

I also see kindness in her smile instead of just menacing teeth. I jokingly tell my

mom that I am becoming a gringa, because of my current growing toleration for

dogs.

Prisca Dorcas Mojica Rodriguez is a chonga Mujerista from Managua, Nicaragua currently living in Miami, FL. She recently graduated with her Masters from Vanderbilt University, and is looking to take some much needed time off to refresh. She is also the founder of Latina Rebels. Her interests are within biopolitics as it relates to Latina embodiment, specifically concerning models of conquerable flesh which dictate that the naturalization of womyn’s bodies directly impacts entire countries and peoples. Thus her work is around reclaiming and upholding embodied resistance, particularly within chonga and chola subcultures. Que viva la mujer!
