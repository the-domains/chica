Power in Numbers

Representing an important segment to the American fabric, Latinas, women of color and the vote.

Women of color constitute more than half of vote-eligible people of color at 53 percent—a staggering 35 million eligible voters. In the past two years, more than 2 million women of color have joined the vote-eligible population.

Latinas in Arizona experienced a VEP increase of 69.1 percent, or 244,000 more vote-eligible Latinas since 2000. And in every cycle since 2002, Latinas have comprised a larger share of the electorate in midterm elections than in presidential elections—a clear indication of their potential voting strength in future state and local elections. This shouldn’t be taken lightly, the implications here are HUGE!

But don’t take my word for it; really it’s all in the data. Recent elections validated that women of color are a key, surging voting bloc. In 2008, 2012, and 2013 elections indicated that women of color, when grouped in a monolithic group (we do this for the sake of common political goals here) and can significantly affect electoral outcomes. That’s right, you heard it. When grouped together, we have more power than they would like us to know. I cannot make these things up. Politicos, strategists, pundits, media and candidates alike rarely take stock in women of color as distinct political agents, with specific priorities, political inclinations, and voting behaviors. If and when women of color are considered at all, they are more often than not grouped commodiously with women, youth, or a specific racial or ethnic group. I don’t think this is necessarily a coincidence, what say you?

While not a homogenous group, but for the purpose of data and political strength given within this, women of color comprise an important demographic that can, when acting in unison on common issues, create an incisive arena, not only on elections, but in general public policy as well. This impact is particularly centric at state-level. What does that mean? That means we have the key to potentially push back collectively and maybe, define Congress? Pretty sure I’m not the only one that has had quite the bitter taste of the Mad Hatter Tea Partyers running the show. A looming chance of yet another sequester (you know, when the politicians don’t know how to play well in the sand box and end up shutting down the whole government), the madness of Boehner trying to jump the Titanic ship that is our current GOP, and even failed at that. I think you get my point… enough!

Women are the largest voting blocs in the U.S., and women of color are the fastest-growing segment within this group. So why are we not talking about this more? Our common issues are irrefutable; there is strength in numbers, no doubt. Let’s not let the political elite keep us like puppets and start to recognize, talk about and spread the word of the power we have here.

Fully substantiating the political presence that women of color represent requires discerning who we are, knowing what issues are most important to us, and identifying how best to motivate in consistent participation.

This unequivocal majority is and will be increasing be a powerful force in the electorate. In states with the highest numbers of vote-eligible women of color, this is where our stakes are highest: Hawaii, New Mexico, California, and Texas. For example, California has experienced the largest increase in real numbers of any state—2.6 million more women of color since 2000—followed by Texas at 1.7 million.

Again, this new reality becomes apparent when one considers that women of color are the fastest-growing segment of the country’s largest voting bloc: women.

Women voting patterns are stronger than our male counterparts; we have a higher voter turnout and average higher rates and numbers. Women voters played key roles in gubernatorial races across the country, and in seven high-profile Senate seats.

Fastest-growing segment of the women’s vote

Women of color represent 74 percent of the growth in eligible women voters since 2000. While the voting-eligible population, or VEP, of white women has increased by less than 6 percent—about 4.4 million eligible voters—over the past eight election cycles, the VEP of women of color has increased by nearly 10 times that at 55 percent. This translates into more than 12 million new eligible women-of-color voters.

Representing almost 90 percent of the overall VEP growth for women since 2012.

Latinas—who represent nearly half the growth of women of color’s VEP and almost 35 percent of the growth of the total VEP for women of all races—realized an increase of more than 82 percent, or 5.9 million additional vote-eligible Latinas since 2000.

Black women’s VEP has grown by 20.6 percent since 2000, adding 2.6 million voters. With more than 15 million eligible black women voters today, black women represent the largest share—43 percent—of vote-eligible women of color and 13.4 percent of all vote-eligible women.

The key is to educate the young generation that is quickly becoming of age to vote, and motivate our vote. I say we push like our lives depend on it, because, well. They do.

In the wake of what seems to be a mass of polarizing ideals spurn from the rise of attention to recent hate crimes and inequality, and the victory of equal rights in marriage, our cultures, heritage and calls for equality have surged a necessary wave of movements to be re-energized in a call to end the patriarchy and platitudes that have historically dictated this country.

The Black, Latino, Feminist and LGBTQI+ Movement call for rightful representation, equality, respect, dignity, freedoms, an end to discrimination and end to the historical suppression of our communities – with these movements, there’s a common, yet invisible, thread that (should) binds us.

If we turn back the dial 40 years, we had a similar occurrence, when we saw activists such as the likes of Gloria Steinem, Martin Luther King, Jr., The Black Panthers and Cesar Chavez (to name just a couple) all creating important and progressive movements, which led to monumental legislations, enactments and SCOTUS rulings.

This was under Lyndon B. Johnson’s leadership of this country, and much of this came because one white politician whose personal beliefs coincided with that of the people of the nation. And those that didn’t feel that way, he was able to persuade them by riding the coat tails of JFK’s legacy. He did this by masking his initiatives, as JFK’s will, at a time when our country was mourning the great champion JFK. Thereby, anything attached to JFK was summarily approved, allowing progressive agendas to move forward.

Huey Newton made an important point during one of his speeches (Huey Newton was the founder of the Black Panthers, an African-American civil right group).

“Whatever your personal opinions and your insecurities about
homosexuality and the various liberation movements among homosexuals
and women (and I speak of the homosexuals and women as oppressed
groups), we should try to unite with them in a revolutionary fashion….We must gain security in ourselves and therefore have respect and
feelings for all oppressed people.”

Recently we’ve seen great strides and great sadness. Seven AME churches being burned in the South, pretty much at the same time the Supreme Court ruled in favor of marriage equality, and the confederate flag finally (only 150 years later) coming down, undoubtedly marks a very marked time in our history- and a very divided nation.

During this same month, a white supremacist presidential hopeful spewed bigoted remarks in his presidential candidacy announcement, el Pato Trump, while a brave young woman, carrying a mattress to her graduation day at Columbia University in protest of her college rape tragedy, and the inactions of so many. A brave Bree Newsome climbed a flagstaff in South Carolina to bring down the confederate flag, down. The same time when anti-abortion laws affecting sex trafficking victims and other victims were passed just weeks before that, on the anniversary of Roe v Wade. Only in America could we set one foot forward towards progress, while taking two steps backwards in the vile battleground of inequality in such a short and what seems like, a very devise time in our nation. - And while we celebrated our victories and unions, we also hung our heads down in disbelief in a deep sorrow. A do nothing Congress and Sleeping Senate has done nothing to help move us forward, or focus on policy rather than politicking.

The movements of the 60’s and 70’s showed us a great many things that we have learned from; that we can cause change be rallying together, by calling out the status quo and not being complacent with the patriarchy we’ve been bamboozled with in politics. But what went wrong? What happened to progress? This soon took a turn back during the 80’s – when the rise of the AIDS epidemic sprung up the fear mongering –a xenophobic administration that ignored the devastation that was happening to our own in the land of the free, America. Ignored because the rise of another oppressed community was flourishing- the gay community. It wasn’t until 20,000 people died before President Reagan even muttered the word “AIDS’ under his breath. His willful ignorance and disdain for the gay community was such. This was a marked moment of retreating in our great strides in, what had been, a progressive moment in time. The time the GOP took control and our progress.

Today, we stand in the face of hate yet again. Their are a couple of key factors to the rise of un-censoring and revolt. Certainly, the Occupy Wall Street movement can claim a pivotal role, and with such we see when the oppressed starts to speak out, fear rises and the suppressor lashes out.

There is an important factor giving way to today’s revolutionaries: The age of technology. With our ingenuity we have crafted a digitally connected world, where the dissemination of information moves faster than ever – giving light to these incidents as they happen from coast to coast, and allowing us to analyze the reality of the comprehensive oppression, suppression and overall hate spurred discrimination that happens, appallingly, to every minority group in every corner of the nation.

We are the most connected and engaged, tolerant and progressive thinkers since the 60’s and 70’s – with one touch of sophistication and advantage that is a blow to the oppressors of today – and that is technology, digitalization and social media.

With the creation on these new advancements, the union of our movements now is especially important. We have created intersectionality before, but I will be the first to admit it’s lacking today, and we should band together like, well, our lives depended on it. The discrimination plague drives us into two active groups – those that perpetuate and those that fight in protest. We must keep in mind that the progress of one group alone allows the gaps in disjointed movements to crack open- and give way to intolerance to rear its ugly head down the line. So let’s take a chapter from history, and use that to improve the Movement and make it one, in solidarity.

What does history have to teach us this time around? Unity. Not within each movement, but extending our hands to everyone that stands for equality. For the advancement of one while not the other helps only for a short term, and will bring complacency back again to any sort of discrimination. Let’s not let that happen… again.
